    Research Experience

    1. Application of Hyperdimensional Computing in Provenance graph based Intrusion Detection System

      August 2025 - Present

      Cybersecurity

      Intrusion Detection System

      We are exploring the application of hyperdimensional computing to enhance the efficiency and effectiveness of provenance graph-based intrusion detection systems. Provenance graphs are a powerful tool for tracking the lineage of data and can provide valuable context for detecting anomalies. However, traditional methods such as Graph Neural Networks are usually computationally expensive, making them impractical for real-time application and in resource-constrained devices. On the contrary, hyperdimensional computing allows parallel vector operations by design, which makes it a powerful tool for optimizing the training and inference time. By leveraging the unique properties of hyperdimensional vectors, we aim to improve the speed and accuracy of anomaly detection in these systems. At present, we are considering DARPA E5 datasets (CADETS, OPTC and TRACE) for our experiments.

    2. Reassessing Prompting Paradigms: The Role of Traditional Methods in Reasoning-Capable LLMs

      February 2025 - Present

      Machine Learning

      LLM

      Prompt Engineering

      In this research, we aim to evaluate the effectiveness of traditional prompting methods in the context of recent reasoning-capable large language models (LLMs). Over the past few years, prompting methods such as chain-of-thought, self-reflection, program-of-thoughts etc. provided significant performance gains over direct prompting on multiple benchmarks. However, the landscape of LLMs is rapidly evolving since the reasoning-capable LLMs have arrived. With the rapid advancements in reasoning capabilities, it is crucial to understand how well-established prompting techniques perform with these new models. We are assessing various reasoning benchmarks using frontier LLMs such as DeepSeek-R1, OpenAI o-series, Qwen etc. Our goal is to provide insights into the strengths and limitations of traditional prompting methods when applied to state-of-the-art LLMs, on mathematical and general benchmarks. My supervisor in this project is Prof. Dr. Rifat Shahriyar. We are collaborating with Dr. Md Rizwan Parvez . We are currently preparing a manuscript for submission in a peer-reviewed conference.

    5. SwarmPrompt

      February 2025 - Present

      LLM

      Metaheuristics Algorithms

      Prompt Optimization

      In this project, we have developed a prompt optimization framework called SwarmPrompt, a framework that integrates Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) for automated prompt optimization. PSO is a computational method inspired by the social behaviors of organisms such as bird flocking and fish schooling. It optimizes problems by iteratively improving candidate solutions based on a measure of quality. In the context of prompt optimization, PSO enables efficient exploration and exploitation of the search space, facilitating the discovery of high-quality prompts that enhance model performance. Unlike traditional PSO, SwarmPrompt operates in a semantic search space, where prompts evolve via structured social exchange. To evaluate the effectiveness of SwarmPrompt, we have applied it to the ASSET dataset, a benchmark designed for tuning and evaluating sentence simplification models with multiple rewriting transformations. On ASSET, SwarmPrompt achieved a 55.53 SARI score, surpassing the current Differential Evolution baseline by 17.1%. We hope to publish our findings in a peer-reviewed conference soon.

    • Machine Learning

      Reinforcement Learning

      OpenAI Gymnasium

      RL-Pacman

      [Code] As the part of our Machine Learning course project, I developed RL-Pacman along with a friend of mine. It is a reinforcement learning agent that learns to play the classic Pacman game using Double Deep Q-learning algorithm. We have used OpenAI Gymnasium for game logic implementation and interaction. Our RL model consists of two CNN's (a policy and a target) that are used to predict Q values for each move given a "state" from the game. A "state" is defined as a sequence of 4 frames as seen from the game screen. We have made the model play many games or episodes. To choose action at a given state, we have used an epsilon threshold to choose random action with decreasing probability. Otherwise the policy network is used to choose a move. We have stored states, actions, rewards, next states as a tuple in a "replay buffer" of a fixed maximum size deque. Periodically (after few frames) we train our two CNN's on a random sample from the buffer.
